# Memorability choice task pilot (n=20)

In this task, subjects picked cards from two decks, and were told that one deck was lucky.
In fact, the decks both had expected values of 0.5, with probabilistic rewards.

However, in one deck ("memlucky" in the data), all high-value (>0.5) cards had images with high image memorability.All the low value (<0.5) cards had images with low image memorability. On the other deck, the high-value cards had low memorability images, and the low-value cards had high memorability images.

High- and low-memorability images were selected from the [MEMCAT dataset](https://gestaltrevision.be/projects/memcat/) with pre-collected memorability data.

My hypothesis was that, if people inferred the value of the decks by sampling from memory, they would be biased towards the deck with high-value, high-memorability cards (they would retrieve these cards more because of their memorability, and thus their estimate of the deck's value would be inflated). 

In fact, with only n=20, there was mild support for this in the data (see "memchoice-data.png", lower right panel). People selected from this deck slightly more than 50% of the time.

This task would be good to revisit with value-associability instead of image memorability!
